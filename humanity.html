Ethnic Introduction

Overview

In the People's Republic of China, all 55 legal ethnic groups, except for the main ethnic group Han, are ethnic minorities. After the founding of New China, the Party and the government began to order the organisation of efforts to identify ethnic names and groups in order to formulate and implement the work of national unity and unification and also to enable all ethnic groups to realise their equal rights.

As a result of these efforts, scientists have sorted out the basic composition of our ethnic family and identified the 56 ethnic components. The successful identification of ethnic groups has laid the foundation for the basis for carrying out ethnic work.


Under the leadership of the Communist Party of China, some ethnic minorities whose social forms were still at the end of primitive society or under the slave system or feudal serfdom before the founding of New China have crossed the historical divide of hundreds or even thousands of years in just a few decades and have entered modern society. In May 1947, China established the Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region, the first ethnic autonomous region at the provincial level under the leadership of the Chinese Communist Party.


Although the population of China's ethnic minorities is small, they are widely distributed. All provinces, autonomous regions and municipalities directly under the Central Government have ethnic minorities living in them, and the vast majority of county-level units have more than two ethnic groups living in them. China's ethnic minorities are mainly found in Inner Mongolia, Xinjiang, Ningxia, Guangxi, Tibet, Yunnan, Guizhou, Qinghai, Sichuan, Gansu, Heilongjiang, Liaoning, Jilin, Hunan, Hubei, Hainan, Taiwan, Chongqing and other provinces and autonomous regions. The province with the largest ethnic composition in China is Yunnan, with 25 ethnic groups.
